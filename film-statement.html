Who I am as a filmmaker

Who I am as a filmmaker

I am a freshman filmmaker working in dramatic, character-driven stories. I began to work in film less than a semester ago, in Dr. Bradley Hammer's ENG 256, and I've loved exploring how staging and props can drive a narrative. The films I want to make put relatable characters in tense situations and let the props and the distinct motives drive the story.

The thread between my works

Across Hit Me, Gimghoul or Nothing, Setting Things Straight, and my photo essay, the same question keeps surfacing: how much agency do people have, and what do they do with it? The gambler in Hit Me is dying of liver failure but keeps drinking. Camden in Gimghoul or Nothing is so lonely he lets himself be manipulated by people he barely knows. Naomi in Setting Things Straight has to decide whether her faith or her friendship comes first. The man in my photo essay sits with a glass of whiskey that will neutralize the antibiotics keeping him alive. Each character has been put in a situation against their will but must now make a choice.

Genre

I work in dramatic narrative, often with moments of dark comedy. I want the audience uncomfortable. What is uncomfortable in real life is often what is funny and interesting. I am most interested in situational archetypes like the fall, death and rebirth, and the battle of good versus evil, because those are the situations that test a character's ability to make good choices.

Directors and writers I follow

Cinematographically, my biggest influence is the Safdie Brothers' Uncut Gems. I enjoy the close shots, the refusal to let the viewer breathe, and the sense that the camera is inside the situation. That is what I want to do when I have control over the cinematography. I prefer visceral close shots over wides because closeness forces the audience to commit. Larry David and Tina Fey are my favorite writers. David for the discomfort and the willingness to sit in a moment until it gets unbearable. Fey for sharp dialogue and characters who stay funny even when the situation is serious.

Writing and character development

I start with the social commentary, then build the characters' motives around it. The strongest films I have worked on have had conflicting and coalescing motives, where a character wants two things that cannot both be true. Ruth and Naomi in Setting Things Straight are the clearest example: Naomi's faith pulls her one way; her love for her friend pulls her another. Once the conflict is in place, I layer in allusions and foreshadowing.

Cinematography

I'm a big fan of mise-en-scène. Every prop should function as a plot device, rather than a decoration. In Hit Me, the rose was a symbol of death, the water glass revealed the protagonist's illness, and the cigars showed the allure of debauchery. When I am behind the camera, I push for tight shots that put the viewer directly inside the scene.

Sound

Sound is where I set tone before the dialogue does. On the Setting Things Straight trailer, I built the audio bed around an original cover of the Indigo Girls' "Closer to Fine" because the idea of searching for certainty mirrored the conflict between Ruth and Naomi. Music reinforces everything the character and props are saying. In the trailer I made for Gimghoul or Nothing, intense breathing and fast music created a visceral intensity.

Lighting

I prefer a mix of low-key and high-key lighting that can emphasize key details or moments. The casino in Hit Me and the darker areas in the photo essay both use light to focus the audience on what matters. I want the viewer's eye drawn to one thing in the frame at a time, and lighting is the cleanest way to direct it.
