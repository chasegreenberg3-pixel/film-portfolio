About Me

The filmmaker behind the lens

My name is Chase Greenberg. I'm a freshman at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, studying Economics with minors in Business and Civic Life and Leadership. I first discovered filmmaking through Dr. Bradley Hammer's class ENG 256: Crafting the Dramatic Film. Before this class, I didn't have a vocabulary for or understanding of what makes a story good. Now I can see how every line, character, sound, or action contributes to a film and to its core storylines and archetypes.

As a filmmaker, I'm drawn to characters dealing with choice and free will. For the film Hit Me, I took on the role of visual architect. I brought most of the props to set, including the flower, the poker table, the whiskey-colored liquid, the cigars, and the green screen, and I pushed to make each one serve more than one purpose. To convey his illness, the gambler drinks water while the extras drink whiskey and smoke cigars. The rose serves as a historical symbol of death. Every prop was a plot device, encouraging the viewer to question the main character's agency. This film taught me the value of mise-en-scène to drive the story, rather than purely adding to aesthetics.

For Gimghoul or Nothing, I served as scriptwriter and storyboarder. I shaped our thesis, that loneliness drives people to obsession and ultimately makes them manipulable, and I built the plotlines, allusions, and foreshadowing around it. The lesson from this film came before we began production. I scrapped our original squirrel concept mid-development because the premise wasn't rooted in anything real. Strong subject matter is the foundation of a good film. No amount of clever plotting saves a story people can't connect to.

My photo essay taught me that every frame can contain so much information. I layered a bandaged hand, a glass of whiskey, and a spilled bottle of pills in the foreground, with the grim reaper just over my friend's shoulder. The eye moves front to back: consequence, then temptation, then the person choosing, then death waiting. The inclusion of these various elements tells a complex story about the cost of debauchery. No caption or further explanation is necessary.

For Setting Things Straight, as assistant scriptwriter, I learned that conflicting and coalescing motives are one of the core ingredients of a good film. Ruth's sexuality and Naomi's faith were intrinsically at odds, but Naomi also wanted her friend to be happy, and that second motive pulled against the first. This creates a realistic conflict that pulls the viewer in.

One of my biggest cinematographic influences was the film Uncut Gems, directed by Josh and Benny Safdie. My biggest writing influences were Larry David and Tina Fey. I also took inspiration from The Breakfast Club's trailer when I produced the trailer for Setting Things Straight.

Going forward, I want to be more ambitious: multiple interconnected storylines, each with its own archetype, and subject matter that stays relatable even when the story itself is strange or absurd. I want to set something in a visually striking location and let every frame contain meaning. Telling a story through film is immensely difficult. However, with honest writing and a focus on universal archetypes, it is an amazing tool to connect with people. I plan to continue to use and develop that tool.
